Understanding the VA Disability Claims System
10 min read

The VA disability system operates on a principle that surprises most veterans: you do not get rated for conditions the VA finds - you get rated for conditions you claim. The VA does not proactively identify your conditions, gather your records, or advocate for your rating. You must do this yourself, or with professional help.

Critical Fact

The average first disability rating decision takes 4-6 months. The appeals process can take years. File as early as possible - the Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD) program lets you start your claim 180-90 days before separation. Your effective date goes back to your file date, not your decision date.

What Makes a Condition Service-Connected

  • Direct service connection: The condition started during service (injury, illness, exposure)
  • Aggravation: A pre-existing condition was made worse by service
  • Secondary service connection: A condition caused or worsened by a service-connected condition
  • Presumptive conditions: Certain conditions are automatically presumed service-connected for veterans who served in specific areas or were exposed to specific hazards (Agent Orange, burn pits, Camp Lejeune water, Gulf War syndrome)
L2
What to Claim - The Most Commonly Missed Conditions
12 min read

Most veterans drastically underestimate how many conditions they can claim. Claim everything that was affected by your service - not just the things that feel dramatic or obvious.

Rule of Thumb

If you sought treatment for it while in service, if it is in your medical records, or if it was caused or worsened by your service activities, claim it.

Frequently Overlooked Conditions

  • Tinnitus (ringing in ears): The single most common VA disability. Almost universal in veterans with weapon or heavy equipment exposure. Rate: 10% flat. File it.
  • Hearing loss: Goes with tinnitus. Audiogram during service is the documentation. Often rated 0% but establishes service connection for future claims.
  • Sleep apnea: Frequently caused by or related to service - PTSD, weight changes, deployment sleep deprivation. Has strong nexus pathways and rates 50% if CPAP required.
  • GERD / acid reflux: Extremely common secondary condition to PTSD and stress from service.
  • Knee and back conditions: Running, ruck marching, heavy loads - almost universal in infantry and physically demanding MOSs.
  • PTSD and MST: PTSD ratings start at 10% and can reach 100%. Many veterans do not file because of stigma. A 70% PTSD rating alone significantly changes lifetime compensation.
  • Migraines: Can be secondary to TBI, neck conditions, or PTSD. Rated based on frequency and severity.
  • Burn pit / toxic exposure conditions: PACT Act expanded eligibility significantly. Veterans who served in Southwest Asia after 9/11 have presumptive conditions.
L3
Building a Strong Claim - Evidence and Documentation
12 min read

The strength of a VA disability claim is almost entirely determined by evidence. The VA uses a "benefit of the doubt" standard - if the evidence is roughly equal for and against service connection, they are supposed to rule in your favor. In practice, strong evidence is always better than relying on this standard.

The Three Types of Evidence

  • Service treatment records (STRs): Your military medical records. Request a complete copy from the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC) before separating. These are the foundation of every claim.
  • Buddy statements (lay evidence): Written statements from fellow service members describing your condition, how it occurred, or how it affected your performance. These carry significant weight and are underused by veterans.
  • Private nexus letters: A statement from your own doctor (not the VA's C&P examiner) connecting your condition to your service. This is the most powerful single piece of evidence in most claims.

The C&P Exam - What to Know

The Compensation and Pension (C&P) exam is conducted by the VA or a contracted examiner to evaluate your claimed conditions. Key facts:

  • Attend every scheduled exam - missing one can result in claim denial
  • Describe your worst days, not your average days - the exam captures your functional impairment
  • Do not minimize. Many veterans understate symptoms out of habit. Describe the full impact on your daily life and ability to work.
  • Request a copy of the exam report afterward. If the examiner's notes do not accurately reflect what you reported, this is grounds for a supplemental claim or appeal.
VSO Representation

You can have a Veterans Service Organization (DAV, American Legion, VFW, etc.) represent you in your claim at no cost. VSO claim representatives know the system and often significantly improve outcomes. Use one.

L4
Ratings, Appeals, and Maximizing Your Outcome
10 min read

Understanding Your Rating Decision

When you receive your rating decision, you have three options: accept it, file a supplemental claim with new evidence, or appeal. You have one year from the decision date to file an appeal without losing your effective date.

The Three Appeal Lanes (AMA System)

  • Supplemental Claim: Submit new and relevant evidence. Best when you have a nexus letter, buddy statements, or medical records you did not include originally.
  • Higher-Level Review: A senior VA reviewer looks at the same evidence without new submissions. Best when you believe the original examiner made a legal error.
  • Board of Veterans Appeals: A Veterans Law Judge reviews your case. Takes longer but offers the most complete review.

100% Rating and TDIU

The 100% P&T (Permanent and Total) rating opens access to CHAMPVA for dependents, free VA healthcare with no copays, property tax exemptions in most states, and significant additional benefits. If you cannot work due to service-connected conditions and your combined rating is 70%+ with one condition at 40%+, apply for TDIU - it pays at the 100% rate even if your combined rating is lower.
