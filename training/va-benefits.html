The VA System Overview - What Most Veterans Miss
10 min read

The VA is not one organization - it is a collection of three major administrations: the Veterans Benefits Administration (VBA), the Veterans Health Administration (VHA), and the National Cemetery Administration. Most veterans only interact with one or two of these and miss significant benefits available through the others.

Most Missed Benefits

Vocational Rehabilitation (VR&E / Chapter 31), which pays for school AND a living stipend for veterans with service-connected disabilities, goes unused by hundreds of thousands of eligible veterans every year. If you have any service-connected rating, look at this first.

The Timeline That Matters

  • 180 days before separation: File your disability claim through the Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD) program. Your claim starts processing while you are still in.
  • Day of separation: Apply for healthcare enrollment if you have not. Enrollment is not automatic.
  • Within 1 year: File for any additional conditions you did not include in your BDD claim. The 1-year mark is a key deadline for some benefit timelines.
  • TRICARE ends: Typically 180 days after separation for active duty. Know your coverage gap window.
L2
Disability Claims - How to File and Win
15 min read

The VA disability system is a claims-based system - you do not get rated for conditions you do not claim. This is the single most important fact about VA benefits. The VA will not automatically identify your conditions. You must claim every condition that was caused or aggravated by your service.

What to Claim

  • Every condition documented in your military medical records
  • Every condition you sought treatment for, even informally
  • Hearing loss and tinnitus - extremely common and frequently overlooked
  • Sleep apnea - often service-connected to sleep deprivation, PTSD, or weight changes from service
  • Mental health conditions - PTSD, anxiety, depression, MST-related conditions
  • Secondary conditions - conditions caused or worsened by a service-connected condition
Secondary Conditions

If you have a rated knee condition that causes you to walk differently, your resulting back condition may be secondary to the knee. Secondary service connections can add significant rating percentages. Always think about what your rated conditions cause.

Nexus Letters

The weakest claims lack a nexus letter - a statement from a qualified medical professional connecting your condition to your service. The VA's exam (C&P exam) is not designed to give you the benefit of the doubt. A private nexus letter from your own doctor, structured correctly, dramatically improves claim outcomes.

The Combined Ratings Table

VA disability ratings do not add linearly. A 50% rating and a 30% rating does not equal 80% - it equals 65% (rounded to 70%). Understanding the combined ratings table helps you identify which additional claims have the most impact on your final rating and compensation.

Key Threshold: 100% and TDIU

Veterans who cannot work due to service-connected disabilities may qualify for Total Disability based on Individual Unemployability (TDIU), which pays at the 100% rate even if your combined rating is lower. This is one of the most underutilized benefits in the VA system.

L3
Education Benefits - GI Bill vs VR&E vs ROTC
10 min read

Veterans have access to multiple education benefit programs that can be combined or sequenced strategically. The wrong choice can cost tens of thousands of dollars.

Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33)

Covers tuition up to the in-state public university rate, a housing allowance based on the ZIP code of your school (not where you live), and a book stipend. If your school's tuition exceeds the public rate, the Yellow Ribbon program can cover the gap at participating private schools. Use the school with the highest BAH rate ZIP code in your target city.

Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment (VR&E / Chapter 31)

If you have any service-connected disability rating, VR&E pays for school AND provides a subsistence allowance on top - and it does not use your GI Bill entitlement months. This is almost always the better choice if you qualify. Apply first, then fall back to GI Bill if you do not qualify.

Comparing Your Options

Decision Framework
Use VR&E if you have ANY service-connected rating

Pays tuition + subsistence allowance. Does not consume GI Bill months. Often better total value.

Use Post-9/11 GI Bill if no disability rating or ineligible for VR&E

Maximize by selecting school near highest-BAH ZIP code. Use Yellow Ribbon for private schools.

L4
Healthcare, Home Loans, and Other Key Benefits
8 min read

VA Healthcare Enrollment

Enrollment is not automatic - you must apply. Your priority group determines copays. Veterans with service-connected conditions, low income, or Purple Heart recipients are in higher priority groups with lower or zero copays. Enroll as early as possible even if you plan to use other insurance - having VA coverage as a backup costs nothing and gives you access to the VA pharmacy, mental health services, and specialist care.

VA Home Loan

The VA home loan has no down payment requirement, no private mortgage insurance, competitive rates, and a funding fee that is waived entirely for veterans with any service-connected disability rating (even 10%). If you have a rating, you save $5,000-$15,000 in funding fees on a typical home purchase. Use it.

Pension vs Compensation

VA Disability Compensation is for veterans with service-connected disabilities - income is not considered. VA Pension is a needs-based benefit for wartime veterans with low income and limited assets. These are separate programs with different eligibility requirements.

Benefits Too Many Veterans Miss

  • CHAMPVA - healthcare for dependents of 100% P&T rated veterans
  • Life insurance conversion - SGLI to VGLI within 1 year 120 days of separation
  • Property tax exemptions - varies by state, often substantial for 100% rated veterans
  • Free national park access via America the Beautiful pass for 100% disabled veterans
  • Free hunting and fishing licenses in most states for disabled veterans
