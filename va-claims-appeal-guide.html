VA Appeals Guide 2026

VA Claims Appeal Guide

Denied or low-balled on your VA disability claim? Learn exactly which appeal lane to choose, what forms to file, and how to win your appeal.

VA Claim Checklist

Understanding the VA Appeals Process

When the VA denies your disability claim or gives you a lower rating than you expected, you have the right to appeal. Since the Appeals Modernization Act (AMA) took effect in 2019, veterans choose from three distinct "lanes" to challenge a decision. Each lane has different rules, timelines, and strategic advantages.

You generally have one year from the date of your decision letter to file an appeal and preserve your effective date. Miss that window, and you may lose months or years of retroactive pay.

Key Deadline

Your one-year clock starts from the date on the VA decision letter, not the date you received it. File early to give yourself margin for mailing delays or processing errors.

Lane 1: Higher Level Review (HLR)

What It Is

A Higher Level Review asks a more senior VA claims adjudicator to re-examine your existing evidence. The reviewer looks for "clear and unmistakable error" or any instance where the original rater misapplied the law, ignored evidence, or made a factual mistake.

When to Use HLR

  • The VA ignored or misread evidence already in your file
  • The rater applied the wrong diagnostic code or rating criteria
  • Your C&P exam was inadequate and the examiner's opinion contradicts the medical records
  • You believe the VA made a factual or legal error

How to File

1
Get VA Form 20-0996Download from VA.gov or file online through the VA's Decision Review portal.
2
Select the issues you want reviewedList every issue you're contesting. You can appeal some issues via HLR and others via a different lane.
3
Request an informal conference (optional but recommended)Check the box on the form. This gives you a phone call with the senior reviewer where you can point out specific errors.
4
Submit within one year of your decisionYou cannot submit new evidence with an HLR. The reviewer only looks at what was already in the file.

Timeline

The VA's average processing time for HLR is approximately 125 days (about 4 months). However, complex cases or high-volume periods can push this to 6+ months.

No new evidence allowed. You cannot submit new medical records, buddy letters, or nexus letters with an HLR. If you have new evidence, file a Supplemental Claim instead.

Lane 2: Supplemental Claim

What It Is

A Supplemental Claim allows you to reopen a denied or underrated claim by submitting new and relevant evidence. This is the most commonly used appeal lane because most denials happen due to missing evidence, not legal error.

New Evidence Requirements

"New and relevant" means evidence that was not previously in your VA file and is relevant to proving your claim. Examples include:

  • A new nexus letter from a private doctor linking your condition to service
  • Updated medical records showing worsening symptoms
  • Buddy letters from fellow service members
  • A private Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ)
  • Service records you recently obtained (personnel file, unit records)
  • A new or updated C&P exam (the VA may order one)

How to File

Use VA Form 20-0995. Submit it with your new evidence attached. You can file online at VA.gov, by mail, or in person at a VA regional office. The VA will review all previous evidence plus your new submissions.

Timeline

Supplemental Claims average about 125-150 days for a decision, though claims requiring a new C&P exam may take longer.

Best lane if you were denied for "no nexus." Get a nexus letter from a qualified medical professional and file a Supplemental Claim. This is the single most effective strategy for overturning denials.

Lane 3: Board of Veterans Appeals (BVA)

What It Is

The Board of Veterans Appeals is a higher authority than the regional VA office. A Veterans Law Judge (VLJ) reviews your case. This lane is best when you've already tried HLR or Supplemental and been denied again, or when your case involves complex legal questions.

Three BVA Dockets

Direct Review

The judge reviews only the evidence already in your file. No new evidence, no hearing. Fastest BVA option. Average wait: 1-2 years.

Evidence Submission

You can submit new evidence within 90 days of filing. No hearing. Average wait: 1-2 years.

Hearing Request

You get a hearing (video, virtual, or in-person) with the judge. You can also submit new evidence. Longest wait: 2-4+ years.

How to File a Board Appeal

Use VA Form 10182 (Notice of Disagreement). Select your docket, list the issues, and submit within one year of your decision. If choosing the hearing docket, the VA will schedule your hearing after your case reaches the front of the line.

Strategic tip: The hearing docket has the highest grant rate because you can present your case directly to a judge and submit new evidence. But the wait is significantly longer. Weigh the timeline against your financial needs.

Decision Flowchart: Which Lane Should You Choose?

When you receive a denial or low rating, ask yourself these questions:

1
Did the VA make a clear error with existing evidence?Yes → File a Higher Level Review (VA Form 20-0996). The senior reviewer can correct factual or legal mistakes.
2
Do you have new evidence to submit (nexus letter, medical records, buddy letters)?Yes → File a Supplemental Claim (VA Form 20-0995). This is the most common and effective path.
3
Have you already tried HLR and Supplemental with no success?Yes → File a Board Appeal (VA Form 10182). Choose the hearing docket for the best chance of success.
4
Is your case legally complex or involves a novel interpretation?Go directly to the Board Appeal. A Veterans Law Judge has more authority to interpret law than a regional rater.

Common Mistakes That Get Appeals Denied

  • Missing the one-year deadline: The single biggest mistake. Set a calendar reminder the day you receive your decision letter.
  • Filing HLR when you need new evidence: If the problem is a missing nexus letter, HLR won't help because you can't submit new evidence.
  • Vague or generic nexus letters: "It is possible that..." is not enough. Your nexus letter must say "at least as likely as not" (50%+ probability).
  • Not requesting an informal conference on HLR: The phone call with the reviewer is your chance to point out exactly where the VA went wrong.
  • Submitting the wrong form: Each lane has its own specific form. Using the wrong one delays your case.
  • Not getting your C-file before appealing: Request your complete claims file (C-file) so you know exactly what the VA has and what's missing.
  • Appealing without representation: VSOs and accredited attorneys significantly improve win rates.

How to Get a VSO or VA-Accredited Attorney

Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs)

VSOs like the DAV, VFW, American Legion, and Wounded Warrior Project provide free claims representation. They assign you an accredited representative who can:

  • Review your claims file and identify errors
  • Help you gather supporting evidence
  • File appeals on your behalf
  • Represent you at BVA hearings

To assign a VSO, submit VA Form 21-22. You can find accredited VSO representatives through the VA's Office of General Counsel search tool at va.gov/vso.

VA-Accredited Attorneys and Claims Agents

Private attorneys who specialize in VA disability claims work on a contingency basis — they typically charge 20-33% of your retroactive pay (not monthly payments). They're worth considering if:

  • Your claim has been denied multiple times
  • The case involves complex legal issues
  • You're heading to the Board of Veterans Appeals or CAVC
  • You have significant retroactive pay at stake

To appoint an attorney, submit VA Form 21-22a. Verify they are VA-accredited through the VA OGC search tool.

Watch out for predatory companies. Some companies charge upfront fees for "claims consulting" without being VA-accredited. Legitimate VSOs are free, and legitimate attorneys only charge from retroactive back pay after winning.

CAVC: Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims

If the Board of Veterans Appeals denies your claim, your last option within the VA system is the U.S. Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims (CAVC). This is a federal court in Washington, D.C.

Key Facts About CAVC

  • Filing deadline: 120 days from the date of the BVA decision (no exceptions)
  • What they review: Whether the BVA made a legal error — they do not re-weigh evidence
  • Attorney recommended: CAVC cases are complex federal litigation; pro se (self-represented) veterans rarely succeed
  • Cost: Most CAVC attorneys work on contingency from retroactive pay
  • Outcomes: The court can affirm, reverse, or remand (send back) the BVA decision
  • Timeline: Typically 1-2 years for a decision

Equal Access to Justice Act (EAJA)

If you win at CAVC, the government may be required to pay your attorney fees under the EAJA, potentially reducing or eliminating what comes out of your retroactive pay.

Tips for a Successful Appeal

  1. Read your decision letter carefully. It tells you exactly why you were denied and what evidence was missing. Your appeal strategy should directly address those reasons.
  2. Request your C-file. File a FOIA request or use VA.gov to get your complete claims file. Review everything the VA has before deciding your appeal strategy.
  3. Get a strong nexus letter. For Supplemental Claims, the nexus letter is often the difference between winning and losing. It must use the magic words: "at least as likely as not."
  4. Don't give up after one denial. Many veterans win on their second or third attempt. Each appeal is a chance to strengthen your case with better evidence.
  5. Consider lane switching. If HLR fails, file a Supplemental with new evidence. If Supplemental fails, go to the Board. You can use different lanes sequentially.
  6. Document everything. Keep copies of every form, every piece of evidence, and every communication with the VA. Track dates and deadlines meticulously.
